### Financial statements

Financial statements tell the story of the business. They give a summary of what happened in the business during the financial period. The financial statements DO NOT tell the owner/managers if what happened was good or better than the previous financial report.

The owners/managers must analyse the financial statements and interpret the results. They must investigate and examine the information according to generally accepted calculations or ratios. All businesses use the same formulae so that businesses can compare their results with those of the previous financial period or with the results of other businesses. Interpretation means finding out what caused changes, improvements or declines in the results. With all the necessary information, good decisions are made.

The following concepts apply to all forms of ownership:

* Profitability
* Liquidity
* Solvency
* Risk and gearing
* Return on investment by owner

The return on owner's equity (net profit as a percentage of owner's equity) is an indication of how the owner's investment earned income. The structure of owner's equity may vary for the different forms of ownership but the same basic formula applies.

#### Profitability

This is the ability to generate income, compared with expenses and other costs incurred.

Gross profit on sales, Shows how much of each rand is left after basic cost of products is deducted.

Gross profit on cost of sales, Compare with the mark-up percentage. Gives an indication of how well the business achieved its mark-up percentage.

Operating expenses on sales, Shows what percentage of each rand received is used to pay expenses.

Operating profit on sales, Shows how much money remains after expenses have been paid.

Net profit on sales, Shows how much of each rand is left over for the owners.

#### Liquidity

This is the ability to pay short-term debts. Compare current assets and current liabilities. Current assets are important to a business because they are assets that are used to fund day-to-day operations and pay ongoing expenses.

##### Current ratio

Current assets: Current liabilities

Norm is 2:1 (R2 available for every R1 debt)

To indicate if the business will be able to cover its short term debts (payable within one year)

##### Acid test ratio

Current assets - Inventory : Current liabilities

Norm is 1:1 (R1 available for every R1 debt)

To indicate if the business will still be able to cover its short-term debts even if the stock (inventories) cannot be sold

##### Stock turnover rate

Cost of sales/ Average stock

Average stock = 1/2 (stock at beginning + stock at end)

Indicates how many times the stock is converted into money with a year. The faster you can sell your stock, the faster you earn profit.

##### Stock holding period

(Average stock × 365 days)/ Cost of sales

Period for which the business has stock available.

##### Debtors collection period

(Average debtors × 365 days)/ Credit sales

Average debtors = 1/2 (debtors at beginning + debtors at end)

Debtors usually have 30 days to pay their debt. If they take longer, the business may not be able to use special deals from suppliers or pay day-to-day expenses.

##### Creditors payment period

(Average creditors × 365 days)/ Credit purchases

Average creditors = 1/2(creditors at beginning + creditors at end)

Creditors usually grant 60 to 90 days. The business should stay within this period to keep a good relationship with suppliers

#### Solvency

This the ability of a business to meet all obligations. If a business is solvent, its assets exceed its liabilities. When a business is insolvent, it means that it can no longer operate and is undergoing bankruptcy.

##### Net assets

Total assets - Total liabilities = Net assets or Owners equity.

##### Solvency ratio

Total assets : Total liabilities

If this ratio is 1:1. It means that the owner's equity is 0.

A = OE + L → R1 = R0 + R1

A low ratio like 1:1 indicates problems.

#### Risk and gearing

This is a measure of a business's financial leverage. It indicates what proportion of equity (own capital) and debt (borrowed capital) the company is using to finance its assets.

Debt - equity ratio (Risk)

No-current liabilities : Owner's equity

Norm : anything below 1:1.

Low risk : Higher equity, lower debt

High risk : Higher debt, lower equity

#### Return on investment

The main aim of a business is to make a profit. Does the investment in the business earn more than in any other investment?

##### Partner's earnings

Interest on capital + Salary of partner + Bonus to partner + Share of remaining of profit

##### Partner's owner's equity

Capital of partner + Current account of partner

##### Return on partner's equity

(Earning × 100)/ Average owner's equity of partner

Average owner's equity of partner = 1/2 (Owner's equity at beginning + owner's equity at end)

##### Return on equity for business

(Net profit for year × 100)/ Average total equity
