##### Theorems relating to parallel lines 

In plane or Euclidian Geometry parallel lines are a constant distance apart, and do not meet  or intersect.

A line that cuts or intersects parallel lines at two different  positions is called a transversal.

DIAGRAM: a parallel lines, CD situated above EF are cut by a line forming 4 quadrants around each point of intersection. Line CD and the transversal form angle p on the top left side and angle q on the top right side. They form angle t on the left bot side and angle u on the right bottom side. Line Ef and the transversal also form a quadrant of angles; r on the top left, s on the top right , v on the bottom left and w on the bottom right.

&nbsp;When parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then the following pairs of angles are formed: 

• alternate interior angles t and s; u and r; and alternate exterior angles p and w; q and v.

• corresponding angles q and s; u and w; p and r; t and v.

• co-interior angles u and s; t and r

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then corresponding angles are equal: 

If CD∥EF then p = r and t = v

If two straight lines are cut by a transversal and a pair of corresponding angles are equal, then the lines are parallel: DIAGRAMS

If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then the pair of co-interior angles are supplementary: 

If CD∥EF then u + s = 180° and t + r = 180°

If two straight lines are cut by a transversal and a pair of co-interior angles is supplementary, then the lines are parallel.

##### Theorems relating to triangles

Angle sum theorem

The sum of interior angles of a triangle is 180°: 

P + Q + R = 180°

P, Q and R are supplementary angles. 

The exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the two interior opposite angles: a = b + c

Congruency theorem: Case I

If three sides in one triangle are respectively equal to the three sides in another triangle, the two triangles are congruent.

&nbsp;∆ABC = ∆FED (SSS)

Congruency theorem: Case II

If two sides and the included angle in one triangle are respectively equal to two sides and the included angle in another triangle, the two triangles are congruent. 

∆LKJ ≡ ∆GHI (S∠S)

Congruency theorem: Case III

If two angles and a side in one triangle are respectively equal to two angles and the corresponding side in another triangle, the two triangles are congruent.

∆RQP ≡ ∆ONM (∠∠S)

Congruency theorem: Case IV

If the hypotenuse and a side of one right-angled triangle is respectively equal to the hypotenuse and the corresponding side of another right angled triangle, the two triangles are congruent:

∆XWV ≡ ∆STU (RHS)

Isosceles triangles theorem

If a triangle has two sides equal, the angles opposite the equal sides are also equal.

Base angles and opposite sides of an isosceles triangle are equal.

Angles and sides of an equilateral triangle are equal. The measure of each angle of an equilateral triangle is 60°.

If AB = AC, then ∠B = ∠C 

If a triangle has two angles equal, the sides opposite the equal angles are also equal.

If ∠E = ∠D, the FE = FD 

Pythagoras’ theorem

In a right-angled triangle, the square on the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares on the other two sides.

If ∠B = 90°, then b^2 = a^2 + c^2

If the square of the longest side of a triangle is equal to the sum of the squares on the other two sides, then the angle opposite the longest side is a right angle.

If EF^2 = FD^2 + ED^2, then ∠D = 90°

Midpoint theorem

The line segment joining the mid-points of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and equal to half its length.

If GJ = and GK = KI, then JK∥HI and JK = 1/2HI

The line passing through the mid-point of one side of a triangle, parallel to a second side, bisects the third side.

If ST = TR and TU∥RQ, then SU = UQ

### An introduction to circles

Centre - The middle point that is equidistant from all points on the circumference; usually it is indicated by a dot and a label (e.g. O). A circle is named by its centre (e.g. ʘO).

Circumference - The length of the boundary. It is the circle’s perimeter. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Radius - A segment with one endpoint the centre of the circle and the other endpoint on the circle.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Chord - A segment that joins two points on the circle, that is, from one point on the circumference to another. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Diameter - A chord passing through the centre of the circle.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Segment in a circle -  The area of the circle between a chord and the circumference, where the smaller segment is called the minor segment and the bigger segment is called the major segment.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Arc - A portion of the circumference.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Sector - A region formed by two radii and an arc of a circle.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Tangent - A straight line that intersect the circle in exactly one point.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Secant - A chord extended beyond the circumference on one side.DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Congruent circles - Circles that have equal radii. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Concentric circles - Circles with the same centre. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Inscribed polygon - All vertices of the polygon are on the circle. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

Circumscribed polygon -  All the sides of the polygon are tangent to the circle. DIAGRAMATIC ILLUSTRATION

### The circumcentre of a triangle

Another direct consequence of theorem 1 is that a unique circle can be drawn through any three points provided the points are not on a straight line. In other words, any three points on a plane are concyclic. If a circle passes through the vertices of ΔABC, then ΔABC is circumscribed. The circle is called the circumcircle of ΔABC. 

To locate the centre we perpendicularly bisect any two sides of the triangle. The centre of the circle is called the circumcentre. 

There are three cases to consider in determining the circumcentre:

&nbsp;Inside the triangle if the triangle is acute-angled

&nbsp;Outside the triangle if the triangle is obtuse-angled

&nbsp;On the hypotenuse of the triangle if the triangle is a right-angled triangle.

Example: 

DIAGRAM:A circle centre O

O is the centre of the circle; AC is a diameter and BM = MC.  

Given that the radius is 5 cm, BC = 8 cm and ∠C 37°, show that:

&nbsp;a) ∠B = 90°

&nbsp;b) AB = 2\*MO

&nbsp;c) ∠COM = ∠CAB

&nbsp;Solution

&nbsp;a) OM ⊥ BC (OM bisects BC and O is the centre of the circle)

&nbsp;∴O is the circumcentre of the circumscribed ΔABC

&nbsp;Hence B = 90°   (circumcentre is on the hypotenuse)

&nbsp;b) MO^2 = CO^2 – CM^2  (Pythagoras Theorem)

&nbsp;MO^2 = 5^2 – 4^2  (CO = 5 cm is a radius and CM = 4 cm; given)

&nbsp;∴ MO = 3 cm

&nbsp;AB^2 = AC^2 – BC^2  (Pythagoras Theorem)

&nbsp;AB^2 = 10^2 – 8^2  (AC = 2CO and BC = 10 cm; given)

&nbsp;∴ AB = 6 cm

&nbsp;Thus AB = 2 × MO

c) OM∥AB   ( ∠OMC = ∠ABC = 90° , corresponding angles =)

&nbsp; ∴ COM = CAB   (corresponding ∠s=)

&nbsp;Alternatively,

&nbsp; ∠COM = 90° −  37°   (Sum of interior angles of a triangle)

&nbsp; ∴ ∠COM = 53° 

&nbsp; CAB = 90° −  37°   (Sum of interior angles of a triangle) 

&nbsp; ∴ ∠CAB = 53° 

&nbsp;Thus  ∠COM = ∠CAB   (Both  = 53°)

### Cyclic quadrilateral 

Cyclic quadrilateral (or cyclic quad for short) is a four-sided polygon that is inscribed in a circle, that is, all four vertices lie on the circumference of a circle.

Theorem  

Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral are supplementary, i.e. add up to 180°.

Theorem  

The exterior angle of a cyclic quadrilateral is equal to the interior opposite angle.

### Tangents, secants and chords

An axiom is a statement that we accept as true without proving it.

Axiom: A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius drawn from the point of contact. 

We will use the axiom to establish and explain other relationships that involves tangents to circles. 

Theorem: 

Two tangents drawn from the same point outside the circle are equal in length.

Theorem  

The angle between a tangent and a chord is equal to the angle subtended by the chord in the alternate segment.
