### Using the Cartesian plane to locate points

The position of any point on a Cartesian plane can be specified by means of an ordered pair of 

numbers (x; y) where: 

•	the x-coordinate of a point is the value that tells us how far left or right the point is along the horizontal axis, also known as the x-axis.

•	the y-coordinate of a point is the value that tells us how far up or down the point is along the vertical axis, also known as the y-axis.

### Distance between two points

CASE1- Distance between 2 points with the same x or y coordinates

Calculating the distance between the two points that have the same y-coordinate is equivalent to finding the difference between their x-coordinates.

Calculating the distance between the two points that have the same x-coordinate is equivalent to finding the difference between their y-coordinates.

CASE 2- Distance between 2 points with different x and y coordinates i.e any 2 points.

We can show that on a cartesian plane the length of the 2 points can be drawn as a hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle and the length can then be calculated using Pythagoras’ Theorem.

Distance formula: the general case:

For any 2 points A and B the distance between the points will be given  by:

AB = √\[(y\_B - y\_A)^2 + (x\_B - x\_A)^2]

&nbsp;

### Midpoint coordinates of a line segment joining two points

The point M, halfway between the points A and B, is called the midpoint of the line segment AB. 

We define the x-coordinate and the y-coordinate of the midpoint of a line segment between 

two coordinate points, (x1; y1) and (y2; y2) to be: 

x-coordinate of M = (x-coordinate of A + x-coordinate of B)/2 and 

y-coordinate of M = (y-coordinate of A + y-coordinate of B)/2 .

Therefore, the coordinates of a midpoint of a line segment can be calculated as: \[(x\_1 + x\_2)/2 ; (y\_1 + y\_2)/2].

##### Example

Problem: Calculate the midpoint M, of the vertical line segment AB, where A(−3; 4) 

and B(3; 4).

Solution: 

x-coordinate of M = (−3 + 3)/2 = 0/2 = 0

y-coordinate of M = (4 + 4)/2 = 8/2 = 4 

So, M ((x\_1 + x\_2)/2 ; (y\_1 + y\_2)/2 ) = M(0; 4)

### Gradient of a line segment

One of the most useful quantities that people such as scientists, artisans, engineers, and technicians often use is the gradient, also known as the slope. Usefule for the slope of a roof is called a pitch and when we design ramps. 

We calculate the gradient of a straight line by choosing any two points on the line, for instance A(x\_1 ; y\_1) and B(x\_2 ; y\_2). We do this because we know that for a straight line the gradient is constant.

We define the gradient (m) of a line between two points, as the ratio of the change in the value of y to the change in the value of x, and we write that in fraction form as shown below:

m\_AB = (y\_2 − y\_1)/(x\_2 − x\_1) = change in the value of y / change in the value of x

##### Example

Problem: Find the gradient of the line through the points C(−3; 3) and F(6; −2).

Solution: 

Let (x\_1; y\_1) be (−3; 3) and (x\_2; y\_2) be (6; −2)

&nbsp;m\_CF = (y\_2 − y\_1)/(x\_2 − x\_1) = (−2) − (3)/(6) − (−3) = −5/9

### Parallel lines

Two lines are parallel if and only if:

•	their slopes (gradients) are equal and they also do not lie on top of each other

•	 both lines are vertical (their gradients are undefined) and they do not lie on top of each other

##### Examples

1. Problem: What is the gradient of the line parallel to a line with a gradient of 5?

Solution: Parallel lines have equal gradients, so the gradient of a line parallel to the given line 

will also be 5.

2\. Problem: A straight line passes through the points A(0; 2) and B(3; 5). Another straight 

line passes through points C(13; 13) and D(0;0). Are the two lines parallel to each other?

Solution: 

mAB = (5 − 2)/(3 − 0) = 3/3 = 1

mCD = (0 − 13)/(0 − 13) = −13/−13 = 1

The gradients of the two lines are equal, so the lines are parallel.

### Perpendicular lines

Two lines are perpendicular to each other if and only if:

•	the product of their gradients is −1

•	one line is horizontal (gradient = 0) and the other line vertical (gradient undefined)

If the gradient of a line m = 3. The gradient of the line perpendicular to it is m = -1/3 

##### Examples

1. Problem: What is the gradient of the line perpendicular to a line with a gradient of 5?

Solution: The product of gradients of perpendicular lines is −1. So, the gradient of the other 

line must be − 1/5 because 5 × − 1/5 = − 1.

2\. Problem: A straight line passes through the points A(1; 2) and B(3; 8). Another straight line 

passes through points C (5; 5) and D(−4; 8). Are the two lines perpendicular to each other?

Solution: 

mAB = \[(8) − (2)]/\[(3) − (1)] = 6/2 = 3 

m\_CD = \[(8) − (5)]/\[(−4) − (5)] = − 3/9 = − 1/3 

Because mAB × mCD = 3 × − 1/3 = −1, the product of the gradients of the two lines is −1. 

Therefore, the lines are perpendicular to each other

### Equation of a straight line passing through two points

The equation of a line can take different forms, depending on some of the information we are given about a line. 

We know that the gradient of a straight line is given by:

m = change in the value of *y*/change in the value of *x* = (y\_2 - y\_1)/(x\_2 - x\_1)

On any straight line, the gradient between collinear points will be constant. Therefore, the 

equation of the gradients is given by: 

y − y\_1 = m(x − x\_1), or y − y\_1 = (y\_2  − y\_1)/(x\_2 − x\_1) \* (x − x\_1), or y = mx + c

##### Example

Problem: A straight line passes through the points A(0; 2) and B(3; 5). What is the equation of the straight line that passes through the points?

Solution:

y − y1 = m(x − x1)

mAB = (2 − 5)/(0 − 3) = −3/−3 = 1

y − 5 = 1(x − 3)

y − 5 = x − 3

y = x + 2

OR

y − y1 = (y\_2 − y\_1)/(x\_2 − x\_1) \* (x − x1)

y − 5 = (2 − 5)/(0 − 3) \* (x − 3)

y − 5 = 1(x − 3)

y − 5 = x − 3

y = x + 2

OR

y = mx + c

mAB = (2 − 5)/(0 − 3) = −3−3 = 1

y = 1x + c

3 = 1(5) + c

3 = 5 + c

c = 2

y = x + 2
