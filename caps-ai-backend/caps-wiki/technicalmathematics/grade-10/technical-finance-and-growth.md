### Investing through simple interest

The equation used for simple interest is:

           A = P (1 + in), Where

A is the accumulated value, the final total value of the investment

P is the principal value, the  initial amount invested

i =  interest rate per time unit, here per month, expressed as a decimal fraction

n = number of time units, here number of months, over which interest is calculated

To calculate the total interest accumulated: A-P = Pin

##### Examples

1. How to understand simple interest.

Situation: Mimi’s friend, Modiba, an experienced builder, wants to start his own construction company. He has a bit of savings but needs extra money to buy equipment.

He asks Mimi to loan him R12 500 in his business. He promises to pay her back when his business is up and running. After doing a few calculations, they draw up an agreement for how he will repay her. They agree that they will add 1,75% interest of the loan amount for

each month, to the initial loan, until he repays her. When he repays her, he will owe her the R12 500 plus the total interest up to the end of the current month. Mimi trusts Modiba completely and knows he will repay her everything when he has the money.

From Mimi’s point of view, she is loaning Modiba R12 500 . She represents the situation for herself on a timeline – a number line for time:

Principal Amount loaned 12 500---Amount owed after 1 month =  12500 +12500\*1,75/100----Amount owed after 2 months = 12500 +2(12500\*1,75/100)---Amount owed after 3 months = 12500 + 3(12500\*1,75/100)-----Amount owed after n months = 12500 + n(12500\*1,75/100).

2\. Problem: A sum of money is invested at 12% p.a. simple interest. After seven years, the value of the investment is R23 920. What amount was invested?

Solution:

A = P(1 + in)

The amount invested is the principal P. Make P the subject of the formular:

P = A/(1 + in)

A = 23920, i= 12/100, n = 7

∴ P = 23920/\[1 + 7\*(12/100)] = R13000

3\. Problem: R6 000 is invested using simple interest calculated every month for 30 months. The total interest is R1 125. What is the monthly interest rate as a percent?

Solution 1: Using the formula directly

A = P(1 + in)

Monthly interest rate is I, therefore make I the subject of the formula:

i= (A/P -1)/n

A = P + total interest = 6000 + 1125 = 7125

P = 6000

n =30

∴ i= (7125/6000 - 1) ÷ 30 = 6,25 × 10^-3

Therefore, % interest = 100 × i = 0,625% per month

### Investing through compound interest

Interest is not only charged on the principal, but also on the total interest gained up to the end of the previous time unit. Using the same symbols as before, we get the compound interest formula:

&nbsp;                        A = P(1 + i)^n

The interest part of the total amount owed back will be: 

A − P = P(1 + i)^n − P

= P\[(1 + i)^n − 1]

##### Examples

1. How to understand compound interest.

Situation: Mimi’s friend, Modiba, an experienced builder, wants to start his own construction company. He has a bit of savings but needs extra money to buy equipment.

He asks Mimi to loan him R12 500 in his business. He promises to pay her back when his business is up and running. After doing a few calculations, they draw up an agreement for how he will repay her. They agree that each month he will add 1,75% interest of the money owed as of the previous month  until he repays her. When he repays her, he will owe her the R12 500 plus the total interest up to the end of the current month. Mimi trusts Modiba completely and knows he will repay her everything when he has the money.

From Mimi’s point of view, she is loaning Modiba R12 500 . She represents the situation for herself on a timeline – a number line for time:

Principal Amount loaned 12 500---

Amount owed after 1 month =  12500 +12500\*1,75/100 = 12500(1+0.0175)----

Amount owed after 2 months = 12500(1+0.0175)+\[12500(1+0.0175)]\*0.0175 = 12500(1+0.0175)(1+0.0175) = 12500(1+0.0175)^2

Amount owed after 3 months = 12500(1+0.0175)^3

Amount owed after n months = 12500(1+0.0175)^n

We say Our ‘problems are compounded’ when each problem creates yet more problems. You may be unfortunate to have experienced the compounding misfortune of falling behind in your schoolwork. We’ve all been there!

2\. Problem: A sum of money is invested at 12% p.a. compound interest. After seven years, the value of the investment is R28 738,86. What amount was invested?

∴ 28 738,86 = P(1 + 0,12)^7 so,

3\. Problem: R6 000 is invested using compound interest calculated every month, for 30 months. The total interest is R1 125. What is the monthly interest rate as a percent?

Solution:

A = P(1 + i)^n

P = 6000, 

A = P + total interest = 6000+1125 = 7125

n = 30

i = \[{n}√(A/P)] - 1

=  \[{30}√(7125/6000)] - 1

= 5,74 × 10^−3

Therefore, % interest = 100 × i = 0,574% per month.

4\. Problem: A loan to the value of R15 000 is made, with compound interest charged annually at a rate of 20%. The loan and total interest value are repaid in one instalment and this amounts to R31 104. For how many years was the loan held?

Test n = 2 (1,2)^2 = 1,44 too low

Test n = 3 (1,2)^3 = 1,728 too low

### Hire purchase

Hire purchase is a legal agreement between a buyer and a seller where something is bought by paying it off in regular, usually monthly, instalments. The buyer has full use/access to the item while paying it off.

The American term for this arrangement is clearer: ‘rent to own’. You are renting the item until you own it. Legally, the seller owns the item until the full amount has been paid. However, the buyer has full use of the item while it is being paid off. If the buyer stops paying the regular instalments, called defaulting, then the seller may repossess the item and keep any money paid up to that point.

Sometimes a deposit must be paid at the start. Usually interest is also charged. When interest is charged, it is usually simple interest to make things easier for the consumer. We will always assume that simple interest is used. Often the seller will also include an insurance fee against damage to the item.

##### Example

Problem: A fridge costs R4 990. The seller requires a deposit of 15%. The balance is paid off in 48 monthly instalments. Simple interest is charged at 21,65% p.a. Insurance at 2,5% is charged for each instalment.

A. What is the monthly instalment, excluding insurance?

B. What is the total monthly instalment, including insurance?

C. How much more will the fridge cost than if it were bought cash?

Solution to A:

deposit = 0,15 × R4 900 = R748,50

balance = 0,85 × R4 990 = R4 241,50

accumulated value = P(1 + ni)

= 4 241,50 × (1 + 4 × 0,216 5)          \[48 months = 4 years]

= R7 914,64

monthly instalment = A ÷ 48

= 7 914,64 ÷ 48

= R164,89

Solution to B:

instalment with insurance = 164,89 × 1,025 \[increase the instalment by 2,5%]

= 169,01

≈ R169

Usually the seller will round off the instalment to the nearest Rand.

Solution to C:

total H.P. cost = deposit + sum of all instalments

= R748,50 + 48 × R169

= R8 860,50

So, the fridge will cost R8 860,50 − R4 990 = R3 870,50 more on H.P. than its cash price.Does the result of this calculation shock you? If you don’t believe this, have a look at a shop brochure that offers H.P./credit terms. The interest rate is taken from a well-known chain of shops. Can you see that H.P. is only a good idea if you really need something? Financially, it is very unwise to pay almost double for something you don’t urgently need. Rather save and pay cash for it later. Building wealth starts with being smart with your money.

### Inflation

In economics, the word inflation is used to describe the increase in the cost of goods over time. 

Inflation rate is the percentage increase in the price of an item or service. The mathematics of 

inflation is the mathematics of compound interest: 

inflated value = base value × (1 + inflation rate /100 )^number of years

A 2,5kg bag of mealie meal or a loaf of bread costs more now than it did 5 years ago. The amount and quality of the mealie meal has not changed. We say that its intrinsic value is unchanged. It just costs more. This is inflation at work. The word ‘inflate’ means ‘to expand’ or ‘get bigger’.

As the graph shows, inflation is never constant. It changes from month to month and year to year. We can calculate the effective/average inflation rate over any time using the compound interest equation by making i the subject of the formula:

i =  \[{n}√(inflated price after n years/base price at beginning of n years)] - 1

Deflation-When inflation drops below 0% . This is usually not a good thing as it indicates that productivity is slowing down.

Hyperinflation- When inflation is very, very high, as happened in Zimbabwe recently and in Germany in the 1920’s, we have what is called hyperinflation or runaway inflation. The highest official inflation rate in Zimbabwe was 231 150 888,87% during July 2008. This is a very bad situation because cash cannot keep its value long enough to be useful, e.g. a loaf of bread may cost 125 times more at the end of a week than it did at the beginning of the week. Usually, hyperinflation happens when the economy slows down too much.

‘stealthflation’- an item seems to stay at the same price over a period of a few years. In such a case, it is possible that either the quality of the item or the amount of it has decreased. You may be paying the same price but what you are getting is not the same. 

##### Examples

1. Problem: A loaf of bread costs R10. One year later it costs R10,60.

A. What is the inflation rate for a loaf of bread?

B. What will the loaf of bread cost after another year, assuming the same inflation rate?

C. What will the loaf of bread cost after n years, assuming the same inflation rate?

Solution A:

inflation rate = change in cost / initial cost × 100

= (10,60 − 10,00)/10,00  × 100

= 6%

Solution B:

cost two years later = cost one year later × (1 + 0,06)

= \[original cost × (1 + 0,06)] × (1 + 0,06)

= 10,00 × (1 + 0,06)^2

= R11,24

Solution C:

For each year we multiply by 1,06, so after n years we multiply by 1,06^n. 

So:

cost after n years = original cost × (1 + i)^n or A = P(1 + i)^n

These calculations show that inflation is exponential not linear.

Note: We are assuming no ‘stealthflation’ here and that the intrinsic quality of the loaf of bread is the same throughout.

2\. Problem: The average inflation rate for house prices in a particular suburb over the last five years has been 5%. If the average sales price of a three-bedroomed house is R960 000 at present, what was it five years ago, rounded to the nearest ten thousand Rand?

Solution:

A = 960 000,  P = ?,  i = 5/100 = 0,05, n = 5

A = P(1 + i)^n 

960 000 = P(1 + 0,05)^5

so P = 960 000 /1,05^5

=752 185,12 

≈ R750 000

### Exchange rates

Exchange rate is the ratio of one currency to another. It is constantly changing, not just from day to day, but during the day as well.

If you wish to buy something online from a foreign country, you have to pay for it in the currency of that country. If you travel to a foreign country, you have to pay for everything in their currency. Hence the necessity of exchange rates.

Note: When you go to a bank to exchange one currency to another you have to pay an exchange fee to the bank for the service. This means that you will get less of the new currency than you expect from the exchange rate.

##### Example

Problem: You purchase shoes  online, costing $280. The Rand/Dollar exchange rate is R17 to the Dollar. What will the cost be in Rand?

Solution: Let the price in Rand be x, using ratios:

&nbsp;price in Rand/price in Dollars = x/$280 = R17/$1

therefore x = R17/$1 × $280

= R4760
