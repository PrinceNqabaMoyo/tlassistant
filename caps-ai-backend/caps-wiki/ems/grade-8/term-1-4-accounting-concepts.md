In a business, there are two types of assets. Fixed assets are assets that have a long lifespan, such as buildings, plant and equipment, and vehicles. Current assets are assets 

that have a short lifespan. A short lifespan means the assets can be converted to cash in one year’s time. Examples are money owed to the business by debtors, shares that can be sold and office equipment.

There are also two kinds of liabilities. Long-term liabilities are debts that will take longer than a year to pay off, such as a mortgage bond on a building or financing of vehicles. Short-term 

liabilities are those that can be paid of within one year, such as consumables bought on credit from a supplier. 

Owner’s equity is the owner’s rights to the assets of a business. It includes the capital the owner contributed to the business and any drawings that he or she takes from the business 

for his or her own use.

The relationship between assets, liabilities and owner’s equity is called the accounting equation. The accounting equation looks like this:

assets = owner’s equity + liabilities

### DEFINITIONS OF ACCOUNTS

#### Assets

Assets refer to possessions bought by the business that 

can be used for a long time and will enable the business 

to earn a profit. Assets also include cash. We discuss the various types of assets below.

##### Fixed or Non-Current Assets

Assets that the business usually keeps for longer than 12 months.

Land and Buildings: The premises owned by the business –excluding any property that the business rents

E.g. a laundromat has bought the premises from which it operates

Vehicles: All vehicles owned by the business E.g. the delivery van used by the laundromat

Equipment: Items that are used by the business in order to produce goods/services

E.g. the washing machines and tumble dryers used by the laundromat

##### Current Assets

Assets a business owns which are either cash or are expected to be turned into cash during the next 12 months

Bank: Bank is the record of money in a business which increases when money is earned and decreases when money is spent.

Cash float: Cash in the cash register

Petty cash: A small amount of money that is used in a business, 

e.g. stationery

Trading stock (Grade 9 only): Trading Stock refers to the items (stock) that a retailer sells

Debtors control (Grade 9 only): A debtor is a company or person to whom you sell on credit and they pay their accounts at the end of each month

#### Capital

The owner's initial investment in the business is known as Capital.

Capital refers to the owner’s money used to start a business.

##### Owner's Equity

Owner's financial interest in the business. 

Business owes this amount to the owner. 

Net value of the business

###### Owner's personal accounts

Capital: Amount invested in the business by the owner when starting 

the business

Drawings: Money/stock (goods) that the owner withdraws for their own personal use

###### Income

Current income: Income earned by the business for services rendered, e.g. income earned for laundry services

Commission income: Income earned by the business, e.g. selling a house

Rent income: Income earned by the business for renting out land or buildings

* Owner’s Equity is not limited to the initial investment made by the owner as it changes when the business earns an income.
* Owner's Equity also increases when the business's income increases. Income increases the profit which belongs to the owner. 
* Owner’s Equity also decreases when the business incurs an expense. Expenses decrease the profit which belongs to the owner. 
* Income INCREASES the Owner’s Equity and expenses DECREASE the Owner’s Equity.

#### EXPENSES

Expenses refer to any item purchased by the business that is consumed (used up) in the running of the business. Expenses include items used to generate an income for the business. There are many different expenses that a business can incur. 

Material costs: The components used when rendering a service E.g. detergent used in a laundromat

Wages: The weekly amount paid to employees E.g. the driver who drops off the deliveries of the laundry

Salaries: The monthly amount paid to employees E.g. the office administrator at the laundromat

Advertising: All amounts paid in order to promote the business E.g. placing an advert about the specials at the laundromat in the community newspaper

Water and Electricity: These are 'consumed' by the business E.g. the laundromat will use lots of water and electricity

Stationery: Pens, pencils and paper used in the business E.g. all the paper slips that are attached to a laundry order

Insurance: The monthly cost to insure an asset for a specific time period E.g. insurance on the washing machines, tumble dryers and delivery vehicles

Rent expense: If a business does not own its premises, it may need to rent premises E.g. the rental paid for the shop space used by the laundry

#### Liabilities

Refers to money that the business owes to another person or business.

Often a business does not have enough cash to buy an expensive item; the business will then borrow money to buy the item. The table below shows the various types of liabilities.

##### Long-term liabilities or Non-current liabilities

Long-term or non-current liabilities have to be paid back 

over a period LONGER than 12 months

Mortgage bond: A loan to buy business premises that is usually paid off over 10 – 20 years E.g. the land and buildings from which the laundromat operates

Vehicle finance: A loan to buy the required vehicle/s for a business E.g. the delivery vans used to drop off the laundry

Loans: Money that has been borrowed from a financial institution E.g. the laundromat bought new industrial irons and took out a loan which will be paid off over 5 years

##### Short-term liabilities or Current liabilities

Short-term or current liabilities have to be paid within 12 months or LESS

Creditors control: A supplier from which the business buys on credit E.g. the laundromat buys detergent and softener from Unilever Wholesalers

Bank overdraft: A situation where the bank allows you to spend more money than the balance in your Bank account E.g. the laundromat used the bank overdraft facility as it did not have enough money to pay wages this week

SARS: Money owed to the government: (SARS – South African Revenue Service) on personal income and on profits in the business E.g. the laundromat has to pay tax on the profits earned

### Transactions and Source Documents

A transaction is any financial event that affects a business. This could be making a sale, paying for electricity, or buying equipment.

A source document is the original paper or digital proof that a transaction happened. It's the evidence! You can't record a transaction without a source document.

Common Examples:

• Receipts: Proof of a cash sale or payment.

• Invoices: A request for payment for services you provided.

• Bank Statements: A summary of all money that went in and out of the business bank account

#### Subsidiary Journals

These are the first books where we record transactions from source 

documents. We sort transactions by type into different journals. For a Grade 8 service business, we focus on the two main cash journals.

* Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ): This journal is used to record all money received by the business. If cash comes in, it's recorded here.
* Cash Payments Journal (CPJ): This journal is used to record all money paid out by the business. If cash goes out, it's recorded here.

#### The General Ledger

The General Ledger is the "main book" of accounts. After recording 

transactions in the journals, we post (transfer) the totals to the General Ledger.

Think of the ledger as a set of folders. Each account (like Bank, Sales, Rent Expense, Capital) has its own "folder," usually in the shape of a T-account. This helps us see the total activity for each specific account.

#### The Trial Balance

The Trial Balance is a list of all the accounts from the General Ledger and their final balances.

Its main purpose is to check for mathematical errors and make sure that our accounting equation is in balance. We do this by proving that:

Total Debits = Total Credits 

If they don't balance, we know there's a mistake somewhere in the journals or ledger that needs to be fixed.

#### Financial Statements

These are the final reports that summarize the business's performance and financial position.

#### Income Statement

The Income Statement shows if the business made a profit or a loss over a period of time (e.g., a month). It's sometimes called the "Statement of Financial Performance."

It has a simple formula: Income - Expenses = Net Profit (or Net Loss)

It tells you if the business's operations were successful during that period.

#### Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet shows the business's financial position on a single specific day. It's a snapshot of what the business owns and what it owes. It's sometimes called the "Statement of Financial Position."

It is a statement that proves the accounting equation is true: Assets = Owner's Equity + Liabilities

• Assets: What the business owns (e.g., Bank, Equipment).

• Liabilities: What the business owes to others (e.g., Loans).

• Owner's Equity: The owner's investment and worth in the business.

#### Purpose and Importance of Cash Journals

For a service business (like a hairdresser, lawn-mowing service, or car wash), the Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ) and Cash Payments Journal (CPJ) are extremely important.

##### Purpose: 

Their main purpose is to provide a detailed, day-by-day record of all cash that flows in and out of the business. The CRJ tracks all income, while the CPJ tracks all expenses paid with cash.

##### Importance:

1\. Cash Management: Cash is the lifeblood of a small business. These journals help the owner see exactly where cash is coming from and where it's going, preventing them from running out of money.

2\. Error Control: Recording all transactions daily helps reduce the chance of mistakes or forgetting a transaction.

3\. Decision Making: By looking at the journals, an owner can quickly see if they are earning enough cash to cover their payments. This helps them make smart decisions, like whether they can afford to buy new equipment or need to cut costs.

#### The Foundation: Transactions and Double-Entry

A transaction is any financial action a business takes, like paying for rent or receiving money for a service. Every transaction must be proven by a source document. One of the most common source documents is a receipt, which is your proof that cash was either paid or received.

The most important rule in accounting is the double-entry principle. This principle states that every single transaction has two effects. For everything the business gets, it must give something else. This keeps the accounting equation balanced.

• Example: You buy stationery for $10 cash.

o Effect 1 (Get): The business gets stationery (an expense).

o Effect 2 (Give): The business gives away cash (an asset).

This "give and take" is recorded using debits and credits.

#### The First Books: Subsidiary Journals (CRJ \& CPJ)

Instead of one messy book, we use subsidiary journals to neatly record transactions of the same type. For a service business, the two most important journals are for cash.

##### Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ)

This is the "Cash In" diary. Every time the business receives money, it's recorded here.

• Purpose: To have a daily record of all money coming into the business.

• Key Columns:

\-Document: The source document number (e.g., from a receipt book).

\-Day: The date the money was received.

\-Details: Who the money is from.

\-Analysis of Receipts: Why you received the money (e.g., for Capital contribution or Current Income from a sale).

\-Bank: The amount of money received.

##### Cash Payments Journal (CPJ)

This is the "Cash Out" diary. Every time the business pays money, it's recorded here.

• Purpose: To have a daily record of all payments made by the business.

• Key Columns:

\-Document: The source document number (e.g., from a cheque or receipt).

\- Day: The date the payment was made.

\-Details: Who you paid the money to.

\-Bank: The amount of money paid.

\-Other Columns: Columns for common payments like Wages, Rent Expense, Stationery, etc., to make sorting easier later.

• Example: The total cash received from the CRJ Bank column is posted to the debit side(left side) of the Bank T-account in the General Ledger. The total cash paid from the CPJ Bank column is posted to the credit side (right side).

#### Checking Our Work: The Trial Balance

After all posting is done, we prepare a Trial Balance. This is an internal list of every account in the General Ledger and its final balance.

• Purpose: To check if the debits and credits are equal. It's a test to see if we made any mathematical mistakes during the posting process.

• How it Works: It has two money columns: Debit and Credit. You list all account balances, and at the end, the totals of the two columns must be the same. If they are, you can proceed to the next step.

#### The Final Reports: Financial Statements

These are the final summaries you create to understand the business's health.

##### The Income Statement and Profit

This statement answers one question: "Did we make a profit?" 

It focuses only on income and expense accounts. Profit is what's left after you subtract all your expenses from all your income for a specific period. If expenses are more than income, it's a loss.

Formula: Income - Expenses = Net Profit

##### The Balance Sheet

This statement answers the question: "What is the business worth on this exact day?" 

It's a snapshot of your financial position and proves the accounting equation is true. It uses all the remaining accounts:

Assets = Owner's Equity + Liabilities

• Assets: Everything the business owns that has value (Bank, Equipment).

• Liabilities: Money the business owes to others (Loans).

• Owner's Equity: The owner's share or investment in the business (Capital)
