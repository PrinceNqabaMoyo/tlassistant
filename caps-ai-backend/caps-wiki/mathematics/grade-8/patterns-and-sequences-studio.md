## numeric and geometric patterns

&nbsp;What is different to Grade 7? 

• The range of number patterns are extended to include patterns with multiplication 

and division with integers, numbers in exponential form

&nbsp;• As learners become used to describing patterns in their own words, their 

descriptions should become more precise and efficient with the use of algebraic 

language to describe general rules of patterns

&nbsp;• It is useful also to introduce the language of ‘term in a sequence’ in order to 

distinguish the term from the position of a term in a sequence

&nbsp;• Investigating number patterns is an opportunity to generalize – to give general 

algebraic descriptions of the relationship between terms and their position in a 

sequence and to justify solutions.

examples

&nbsp;a) Provide a rule to describe the relationship between the numbers in this 

sequence: 1; 8; 27; 64; ... .. Use this rule to find the 10th term in this sequence. 

Firstly, learners have to understand that the ‘10th term’ refers to position in the 

number sequence. They have to find a rule in order to determine the 10th term, 

rather than continuing the sequence to the tenth term.

&nbsp;example

&nbsp;b) This sequence can be re-presented in the following table:

1st Row: Position in sequence|1|2|3|4|...|10|

2nd Row: Term                |1|8|27|64|...|?|

&nbsp;Learners have to recognize that each term in the bottom row is obtained by cubing 

the position number in the top row. Thus the 10th term will be 10 cubed or 103, 

which is. Using the same rule, learners can also be asked what term number or 

position will 512 be? If the term is obtained by cubing the position number of the 

term, then the position number can be obtained by finding the cube root of the 

term. Hence, 512 will be the 8th term in the sequence since 3√512 = 8

&nbsp;c) Provide a rule to describe the relationship between the numbers in this 

sequence: 4; 7; 10; 13; ... Use this rule to find the 20th term in this sequence. 

If learners consider only the relationship between consecutive terms, then they 

have to continue the pattern (‘add 3 to previous number’) to the 20th term to 

find the answer. However, if they look for a relationship or rule between the term 

and the position of the term, they can predict the answer without continuing the 

pattern. Using number sentences can be useful to find the rule:

&nbsp;1st term: 4 = 3(1) + 1

&nbsp;2nd term: 7 = 3(2) + 1

&nbsp;3rd term: 10 = 3(3) + 1

&nbsp;4th term: 13 = 3(4) + 1

&nbsp;The number in the brackets corresponds to the position of the term. Hence, the 

20th term will be: 3(20) + 1 = 61

&nbsp;The rule in learners’ own words can be written as ‘3 times the position of the term 

+1’ or 3n + 1 where n is the position of the term.

&nbsp;• These types of numeric patterns develop an understanding of functional 

relationships, in which you have a dependent variable (position of the term) and 

independent variable (the term itself), and where you have a unique output for 

any given input value. 

Kinds of geometric patterns

&nbsp;• Geometric patterns are number patterns represented diagrammatically. The 

diagrammatic representation reveals the structure of the number pattern.

&nbsp;• Hence, representing the number patterns in tables makes it easier for learners to 

describe the general rule for the pattern. 

example

&nbsp;Consider this pattern for building hexagons with matchsticks. How many 

matchsticks will be used to build the 10th hexagon?

Diagram: there are 4 hexagons made by joining together match sticks. The 1st shape has 6 matchsticks, the second 12, the 3rd has 18 and the 4th has 24 match sticks.

&nbsp;The rule for the pattern is contained in the structure (construction) of the 

successive hexagonal shapes: 

(1) add 1 matchstick per side 

(2) there are 6 sides

&nbsp;(3) add 6 matchsticks per hexagon as you proceed from a given hexagon to the 

next one. 

So, for the 2nd hexagon, you have 2 x 6 matches; for the 3rd hexagon you have  

3 x 6 matches: Using this pattern for building hexagons, the 10th hexagon will have 

10 x 6 matches.

&nbsp;Learners can also use a table to record the number of matches used for each 

hexagon. This way they can look at the number pattern related to the number of 

matches used for each new hexagon.

descriptions of the same pattern can be different when they encounter it at 

different stages of their mathematical development.
