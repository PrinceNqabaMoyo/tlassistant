## NOTES AND EXAMPLES

### What is bank reconciliation

Bank reconciliation forms part of the internal control procedure of a business. The business compares their cash records with the information they receive from the bank:



Records of the business:

Cash Receipts Journal, Cash Payments Journal and Bank account



Records of the bank: Bank statement



They investigate any differences and make corrections: they reconcile the records of the business and the records of the bank.



Differences between the records could be due to:

* Different timing in recording the information
* Errors n recording the information



#### Reason for bank reconciliation

* Verifies the amount  in the bank account
* enables business to control cash flow
* provides an opportunity to eliminate errors on the bank statement
* uncovers irregularities.



#### Procedure for bank reconciliation

* Compare the bank reconciliation statement of the previous month with the current bank statement. Mark all the differences (when the information does not appear in both sets of records)
* Compare the amounts in the bank column of the Cash Receipts Journal with the deposits (credits) on the bank statement. Mark all differences.
* Compare the amounts in the bank column of the Cash Payments Journal with the payments (debits) on the bank statement. Mark all differences.
* Investigate items marked on the previous bank reconciliation statement. Make corrections or record them in the current bank reconciliation statement again.
* Record all differences/ items marked on the bank statement either in cash journals of the business or in the bank reconciliation statement if the bank needs to make corrections.
* Record all differences/ items marked on the cash journals in the bank reconciliation statement or correct them in the cash journals.
* Post the new totals of the cash journals in the Bank account in the General Ledger of the business.
* The new balance of the Bank account in the General Ledger should agree with the balance according to the bank reconciliation statement.



Note: The business compiles the bank reconciliation statement- not the bank. The bank reconciliation statement assists the business to keep track of information that the bank has not yet recorded.



##### Possible entries in the Cash Receipts Journal

* Direct deposits on current bank account
* Receipts not recorded/ Entries omitted
* Cancellation of cheques
* Correction of payments (if amount recorded in the Cash Payments Journal was too high)
* Interest received from bank not yet recorded





##### Possible entries in the Cash Payments Journal

* Stop orders or debit orders paid that the bank has paid but the business has not yet recorded
* Bank charges not yet recorded
* Interest on overdraft not yet recorded
* Cheques, received from debtors, dishonoured (RD)
* Correction of payments (if amount recorded in the Cash Payments Journal was too little)
* Electronic fund transfers not yet recorded
* Payments not recorded/ Entries omitted.



##### Possible entries on the bank reconciliation statement

* Outstanding deposits/ deposits not recorded by the bank yet
* Outstanding cheques/ cheques not presented for payment yet
* Correction of errors made by the bank



##### No entry

* When the business receives a post-dated cheque from a debtor, follow this procedure:
1. Record the cheque in the post-dated cheque received register
2. Keep the cheque in a safe place until the date on the cheque
3. Issue a receipt on the date on the cheque
4. Record the cheque in the Cash Receipts Journal
5. Deposit the cheque
* When the outstanding deposits from the previous bank reconciliation statement appear on the current month's bank statement
* When outstanding cheques from the previous Bank Reconciliation Statement appears on the current month's bank statement



##### Other reasons of importance:

•	 For internal control purposes

•	 To identify the correct bank balance and to update records

•	 Deposits made and cheques issued not only affect the business but also the bank concerned therefore these transactions must be verified.

•	 The business keeps a record of all cash transactions in the same way the bank also keeps a record of its transactions with the business, and the accuracy of these transactions must be checked.

•	 The books of the business and that of the bank should agree and the

bank balance should be the same in both books.

•	 Comparisons between the books of the business and that of the bank

must be made on a monthly basis by preparing a Bank Reconciliation

Statement.

•	 Errors, fraud and dishonesty can be detected on a monthly basis.
