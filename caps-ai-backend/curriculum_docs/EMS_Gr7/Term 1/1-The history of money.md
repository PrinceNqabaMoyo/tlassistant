### Traditional societies

What type of money do you and your parents use? Do you

#### Modern societies

A modern society is an industrialised society that uses technology to

produce products for trade.

Most of us use coins, notes and debit or credit cards to pay for

goods or services. We may also use electronic banking such as

Internet banking. When we use this form of payment, we say we

live in a modern society. A modern society is an industrialised

society that uses technology to produce goods for trade. In a

modern society, people have specialised skills. They rely on other

people with different skills to produce goods and provide services

that they cannot make or provide for themselves. For example, if

you are a mechanic, you can fix cars but you cannot treat people

who are sick. If you get sick, you need to pay for medical goods

and services. You trade your services as a mechanic for money,

which you then use to pay other people for their goods and

services.



#### Traditional societies

A traditional society is a society in which communities use basic methods to hunt for and harvest food. Traditional societies lack technology and most of their activities are subsistence activities.



Traditional societies are very different from modern societies.

In a traditional society, communities use basic methods to hunt

for and harvest food. Traditional societies lack technology and

most of their activities are subsistence activities. This means

that they use most of what they produce, and trade very little.

These societies don’t see much economic growth. Traditional

economies are mostly rural and agricultural, and the people who

live in them are self-sufficient. This means that they don’t rely on

others for their survival.



Many communities in South Africa still use the system of lobola today. Lobola is a price that a man pays to his fiancée’s family when he wants to marry her. It is still common for the man to pay this price in cattle.



As you can imagine, money is less important to people in

traditional societies than it is to people in modern societies.



##### Characteristics of a traditional society

Traditional societies = small communities that usually share many of the following features:

1\. decisions are usually made by a senior member of the group

2\. they are self-sufficient and produce goods and foods for their own use

3\. did not use money to buy things

4\. very few possessions and simple standard of living

5\. have a limited but nutritious diet

### Bartering

Bartering is a form of trade in which people exchange

goods without money.

Traditional societies have limitations. The environment in which

subsistence farmers produce goods limits what and how much

these farmers could produce. For example, a sheep farmer in

the Karoo has lots of wool and mutton, but very little timber,

because trees do not grow naturally in the Karoo. A woodcutter

in the Knysna area has lots of timber, but the forests in the area

make it difficult to find good grazing for livestock. Each farmer has something that the other wants. So, the sheep farmer could

trade some sheep for timber to build structures and make fires.

The farmers could exchange their produce in a form of trade that

doesn’t involve money. This is called bartering.

Since the first humans walked the Earth, people have traded with one another for goods and services. The earliest form of money was livestock – farmers would trade cattle, sheep and camels. Later, they exchanged the crops they grew. The items that people used to trade are called commodities. Commodities are items that people use for trade.



##### Advantages of bartering:

1\. It is flexible

2\. Goods are only produced to meet the needs of people so there is no excess or waste

3\. Economic power is not in the hands of a few rich people

4\. Natural resources are only used to meet the needs of people – less waste



##### Disadvantages of bartering:

1\. Difficult to value the various items that are being exchanged 

2\. Everyone is not honest and the products will not have a warranty 

3\. Difficult to find another person who has the product or service 

4\. Difference in the value of the product or service creates a problem 

5\. Large items cannot be cut up to exchange for smaller item of lower value



### Trans-Saharan trade

A good example of bartering in traditional societies in Africa

is the trans-Saharan trade. Between about the 8th and 16th

centuries, countries in Asia and Europe traded with countries

in Africa using trade routes that crossed the Sahara Desert.

Africa produced gold, salt, ivory and slaves. Europe and Asia

produced fabrics such as silk, as well as beads and ceramics.

African, European and Asian communities exchanged these

commodities in a complex barter system. Caravans of camels

carried commodities across the Sahara from areas that are now

Algeria, Libya and Egypt, to the part of West Africa that is now

Ghana, Mali and Nigeria.



Trust was an important part of the barter system. Often along

these routes, and the trans-Saharan trade route in particular,

the trading parties didn’t speak the same language. In a kind

of barter called silent barter, one party would leave their

commodities, such as salt or gold, in a specific place. The other

party would collect these goods, leave their own goods, and

complete the trade without a single word being exchanged.

Silent barter is a kind of barter in which traders exchanged

commodities without speaking or interacting with each other

Bartering has disadvantages, however. People can’t always

get what they want or need by bartering. For example, your

neighbours might not want your extra eggs because they have

their own chickens. If you are bartering food, you can’t store

it for long periods. Also, you would keep your best crops or

livestock for yourself and only trade what you didn’t need. ese

commodities may not be the best quality, so people might not

want them. How do you determine how much timber, for

example, equals the value of ten sheep? e disadvantages of the

barter system eventually led to the development of money as a

trading currency.

#### Promissory notes

Suppose your friend is selling his bicycle for R300, and you want 

to buy it. You only have R200, but you know that your parents 

are going to give you some money for your birthday next month. 

Your friend is happy to let you take the bicycle now and pay him 

the R100 you owe him next month. He asks you to promise in 

writing to pay him the R100 the day after your birthday. So, you 

buy the bicycle for R200 plus a promise in the form of a note. 

Promissory notes are based on this principle.



promissory notes: legally binding notes that buyers use to promise sellers unconditionally that they will pay specific amounts of money within a specific time



A promissory note legally binds the buyer to unconditionally pay a specific amount of money to the seller within a specific time. If you think that promissory notes sound similar to bank loans, you are right. A promissory note is a private version of a bank loan. It declares that the person who issues the note has borrowed money from a private organisation or individual that is not a bank. Just like bank loans, promissory notes can also specify an interest amount on the loan.

Promissory notes date back to the 8th century when they 

were used by the Chinese during the Tang dynasty. Arab traders 

also used them around the 12th century. They were useful to 

traders because it meant that traders didn’t always have to carry 

large amounts of heavy, bulky coins over long distances. It was 

too dangerous. This concept worked so well that promissory 

notes are still in use today.



Promissory notes = a written promise to pay or repay a sum of money at a given time, or on demand. By international agreement, promissory notes must contain: 

1\. the words ‘promissory note’ in the document 2. a promise to pay a definite sum of money 

3\. the time of payment 4. the place where the payment is to be made 5. the name of the 

person to be paid 

6\. the date and the place where the promissory note is issued 

7\. the signature of the person who issues the promissory note



#### Coins

As you saw earlier, bartering has some disadvantages. One of those disadvantages was that it was difficult to determine a commodity’s value. Are a dozen eggs worth two metres of cotton cloth or three metres? Is a sheep worth four chickens or five? How much gold would you need to pay for two sacks of salt? Would the families across the river want to trade salt for your livestock, when they have their own livestock?

One of the ways of solving the problems with the barter 

system was for trading countries to develop currency. Currency is a 

medium of exchange.  It represents or stands for commodities. Instead of meeting trading partners on a trade route or in a local market, traders could now exchange commodities for currency. A trader could sell her basketwork for currency and then use that currency to buy wool, for example. She wouldn’t have to search for a trader who was selling wool, and hope that he wanted to buy her baskets. She also saved a lot of time and effort this way, because she wouldn’t have to cover large distances looking for people to exchange their wool for her baskets.

Very early forms of currency included receipts that represented stored commodities such as grain, and other currency forms such as cowrie shells, ivory and metal ingots. 

Because metal lasted long and had value, traders started to use metals such as gold, silver and copper as currency. Traders exchanged standardised quantities of these metals for commodities that soon developed into coins. Coins were stamped to show that they were the correct weight and value. 

They also indicated the country or area of origin. Traders have been using coins since the time of ancient Greece. Indian traders used coins as far back as 6 BCE.

#### Paper money

Imagine crossing the Sahara Desert or travelling the Silk Road 

with bags and bags of heavy coins for a year’s worth of trading. 

Imagine going to the supermarket to buy a month’s worth of 

groceries and having to pay for your shopping with R2 coins. To 

make trade easier, traders started to use paper money in the form 

of promissory notes and banknotes.

You know that promissory notes are promises to pay a sum 

of money by a specific date. Banknotes are a type of currency 

that represents value. People get paid currency when they do 

certain jobs or sell commodities of value. The money they are 

paid represents the work they have done or the commodity they 

have sold. When people use their salaries to buy a car, a house 

or some groceries, for example, they are actually trading their 

labour for the commodity. The money they have earned for their 

labour represents the work that they have done. So, we say that 

paper money is representative. Representative money is money such 

as paper money that represents the money you have in the bank.



The Chinese started using banknotes in the 7th century, and 

Europe started using them in the 14th century.



First paper money appeared in China during Tang Dynasty = ‘flying money’ . Individuals carried letters of credit with them when they travelled long distances By 15th century there were so many paper notes that they lost their value and the use of paper notes in China disappeared for several hundred years .

1200s AD – Mongol Empire and Asia united and paper money and letter of credit spread to the Islamic Empire and then to Europe .It took another 2 centuries for paper money to spread to the rest of the world. 

1694: the bank of England was established and printing banknotes which became wide spread across the world . Banknotes had face value – you could go to the bank and demand the face value in silver or gold – not anymore today.
