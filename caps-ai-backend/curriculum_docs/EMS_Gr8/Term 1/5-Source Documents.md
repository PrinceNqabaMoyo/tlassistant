## Source Documents



In this topic, learners will learn about FINANCIAL LITERACY. They will look at: • receipts • deposit slips • cash register slips (till slips) • cheques • cheque counterfoils • bank statements • cash invoices

A source document is the original proof that a financial transaction took place. It's the starting point of the accounting cycle because it provides all the details needed to record an event, like the date, the amount, and the reason for the transaction. The rule is simple: no source document, no entry in the books!



#### Receipts

A receipt is a written acknowledgement that a specific amount of money has been received.

• Purpose: To provide proof of payment for both the buyer and the seller.

• The Flow:

-When your business receives cash, you issue the original receipt to the customer and keep a duplicate (copy) for your records. This duplicate is your source document for the Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ).

-When your business pays cash for something, you receive an original receipt. This is your source document for the Cash Payments Journal (CPJ).

#### Deposit Slips

A deposit slip is a form you fill out to deposit cash or cheques into a bank account.

* Purpose: To provide proof that money was physically handed over to the bank for safekeeping in your account.
* The Flow: You fill out the slip and give it to the bank teller with your money. The teller stamps a copy and gives it back to you. This stamped copy is your source document.
* Journal: It confirms the "Bank" column entry in the CRJ.

#### Cash Register Slips (Till Slips)

This is the slip printed by a cash register (or "till") when a customer pays for goods or services.

• Purpose: To act as proof of a cash sale for the customer. For the business, the summary of all the day's slips (the till roll) is used to record the total sales for the day.

• The Flow: The customer gets the original slip. The business keeps the internal electronic or paper roll.

• Journal: The daily total from the till roll is the source document for a single entry for "Cash Sales" in the CRJ.





Cheques and Cheque Counterfoils

A cheque is a written order from you to your bank, instructing it to pay a specific person or business. A cheque counterfoil is the small stub that remains in the chequebook after the cheque is torn out.

• Purpose: The cheque is used to make a payment safely without using physical cash. The counterfoil is the business's crucial internal record of that payment.

• The Flow: The cheque is given to the person you are paying. The business keeps the counterfoil.

• Journal: The cheque counterfoil is the source document for recording the payment in the Cash Payments Journal (CPJ).

#### Bank Statements

A bank statement is a monthly summary from the bank showing all activity in your account.

• Purpose: To check your own records against the bank's records. It also acts as a source document for transactions you might not know about immediately, like bank charges, interest earned, or direct payments from customers.

• The Flow: The bank provides it to the business.

• Journal: Used to record bank charges in the CPJ and interest received in the CRJ.





Cash Invoices

An invoice is a document that lists the goods or services provided, including quantities and 

prices. A cash invoice is specifically for a sale where payment is made immediately.

• Purpose: It's like a detailed receipt, providing more information than a simple till slip.

• The Flow: A seller gives a cash invoice to a buyer when payment is made on the spot.

• Journal:

If you sell something for cash and issue a cash invoice, your copy is a source document for the CRJ.

If you buy something for cash and receive a cash invoice, it's your source document for the CPJ.
