&nbsp;-< 90o (acute angles)

&nbsp;-> 180o (reflex angles)



&nbsp;Constructions

&nbsp;• Accurately construct geometric 

figures appropriately using compass, 

ruler and protractor, including:

&nbsp;-angles, to one degree of accuracy

&nbsp;-circles

&nbsp;-parallel lines

&nbsp;-perpendicular lines



&nbsp;• Constructions provide a useful context to explore or consolidate knowledge of 

angles and shapes.

&nbsp;• Learners have to be shown how to use a compass to draw circles, although they 

might have done this in Grade 6. 

• Learners should be aware that the centre of the circle is at the fixed point of the 

compass and the radius of the circle is dependent on how wide the compass is 

opened up. 

• make sure learners understand that arcs are parts of the circles of a particular 

radius.

&nbsp;• Initially, learners have to be given careful instructions about how to do the 

constructions of the various shapes

&nbsp;• Once they are comfortable with the apparatus and can do the constructions, 

they can practise by drawing patterns, for example of circles or parallel lines.

similarity and congruency

&nbsp;• Similarity and congruency can be explored with any 2D figures.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that two or more figures are congruent if they are 

equal in all respects i.e. angles and sides are equal.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that two or more figures are similar if they have 

the same shape, but differ in size i.e. angles are the same, but sides are 

proportionally longer or shorter. Similar figures are further explored when 

doing enlargements and reductions. Refer to “Clarification Notes” under 3.4 

Transformation Geometry.



&nbsp;solving problems

&nbsp;• Solve simple geometric problems 

involving unknown sides and angles 

in triangles and quadrilaterals, using 

known properties.



&nbsp;solving problems

&nbsp;• At this stage learners can solve simple geometric problems to find unknown 

sides in equilateral and isosceles triangles, and unknown sides and angles in 

quadrilaterals.

&nbsp;• Learners should give reasons for their solutions. 

examples

&nbsp;a) If ∆ABC is an equilateral triangle, and side AB is 3 cm, what is the length 

of BC? Here learners should answer: BC = 3 cm, because the sides of an 

equilateral triangle are equal.

&nbsp;b) If ABCD is a kite, and AB = 2,5 cm and BC = 4,5 cm, what is the length of 

AD and DC? Learners should use the property for kites, that adjacent pairs 

of sides are equal, to find the unknown sides. 



## Geometry of straight lines

&nbsp;Define:

&nbsp;• Line segment

&nbsp;• Ray

&nbsp;• Straight line

&nbsp;• Parallel lines

&nbsp;• Perpendicular lines



&nbsp;• Line segment is a set of points with a definite starting-point and an end-point.

&nbsp;• Ray is a set of points with a definite starting-point and no definite end-point.

&nbsp;• Line is a set of points with no definite starting-point and end-point.

&nbsp;• If two lines on the same plane are a constant distance apart, then the lines are 

parallel. example:

Draw line EF, and GH parallel to each other. The convention to show that they are parallel is to put arrow heads directly on each line pointing the same direction. The  shortform represantation is EF||GH



&nbsp;• If vertical line AO meets or intersects with horizontal line BC at right angle, then 

AO is perpendicular to BC. Be asble to use the shortform way to represent perpendicula lines.

## transformation Geometry

&nbsp;What is different to Grade 6

&nbsp;Learners in Grade 7 have to do transformations on squared paper.



### transformations

&nbsp;• Recognize, describe and perform 

translations, reflections and rotations 

with geometric figures and shapes on 

squared paper

&nbsp;• Identify and draw lines of symmetry 

in geometric figures



#### Focus of transformations

&nbsp;• Using squared paper for transformations allows learners to more accurately 

perform transformations and to compare the shape and size of figures.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that translations, reflections and rotations only 

change the position of the figure, and not its shape or size.

&nbsp;• They should recognize that the above transformations produce congruent 

figures.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that enlargements and reductions change the size of 

figures by increasing or decreasing the length of sides, but keeping the angles 

the same, producing similar rather than congruent figures.

&nbsp;• Learners should also be able to work out the factor of enlargement or reduction.



#### &nbsp;enlargements and reductions

&nbsp;• Draw enlargements and reductions of 

geometric figures on squared paper 

and compare them in terms of shape 

and size





## Geometry of 3d objects



&nbsp;What is different to Grade 6?

&nbsp;• Most of this work consolidates what has been done in Grade 6.



&nbsp;Classifying 3d objects

&nbsp;• Describe, sort and compare 

polyhedra in terms of

&nbsp;-shape and number of faces

&nbsp;-number of vertices

&nbsp;-number of edges



&nbsp;Building 3d model

&nbsp;• Revise using nets to create models of 

geometric solids, including:

&nbsp;-cubes

&nbsp;-prisms



&nbsp;Polyhedra

&nbsp;Examples of sorting or grouping categories:

&nbsp;• cubes (only square faces)

&nbsp;• rectangular prisms (only rectangular faces)

&nbsp;• triangular prisms (only triangular and rectangular faces) 

• pyramids (square and triangular faces)

&nbsp;• cylinders (circular and rectangular faces).

&nbsp;using and constructing nets

&nbsp;• Using and constructing nets are useful contexts for exploring or consolidating 

properties of polyhedra.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize the nets of different solids. 

• Learners should draw sketches of the nets using their knowledge of shape and 

number of faces of the solids, before drawing and cutting out the nets to build 

models.

&nbsp;• The construction of nets is based on the number and shape of faces of the 

solids, and do not require measuring of internal angles of polygons. 

• Learners have to work out the relative position of the faces of the nets, and 

use trial and error to match up the edges and vertices, in order to build the 3D 

object.
