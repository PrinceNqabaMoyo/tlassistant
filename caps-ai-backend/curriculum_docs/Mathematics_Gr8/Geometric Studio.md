&nbsp;similarity and congruency

&nbsp;• This can be explored with any 2-D shape

&nbsp;• Learners should recognise that two or more figures are congruent if they are 

equal in all respects i.e corresponding angles and sides are equal

&nbsp;• Learners should recognise that two or more figures are similar if they have. 

corresponding angles equal and  their sides are proportionally longer or shorter.  

See examples below. 

• Note that in Grade 9 learners will focus on the special cases of similarity and 

congruence in triangles.

&nbsp;• Simiilar figures are further explored when doing enlargements and reductions. 

Refer to “Clarification Notes” under 3.3 Transformation Geometry.

&nbsp;examples: 

• Comparing squares to other rectangles, learners can ascertain that having 

corresponding angles equal does not necessarily imply that the sides will be 

of proportional length. Hence having equal angles alone, is not a sufficient 

condition for figures to be similar.

&nbsp;• Comparing rhombii with sides proportional, learners can ascertain that having 

sides proportional does not necessarily imply that the corresponding angles will  

be equal. So only having sides of proportional length is not a sufficient condition 

for similarity

## Transformation Geometry

### What is different to Grade 7?

&nbsp;• Transformations are done on a co-ordinate plane

&nbsp;• Co-ordinates of points and vertices



### &nbsp;Co-ordinate plane

&nbsp;• Doing transformations on the co-ordinate plane is an opportunity to practise 

plotting points with ordered pairs, and links up with drawing algebraic graphs.

&nbsp;•  Learners have to learn how to plot points on the co-ordinate plane and read 

the co-ordinates of points off the x-axis and y-axis. This is also done with 

algebraic graphs. 

• Learners have to know the convention for writing ordered pairs (x;y)

&nbsp;• Point out the differences between the axes in the four quadrants.



### &nbsp;transformations

&nbsp;• Recognize, describe and perform 

transformations with points on a 

coordinate plane, focusing on:

&nbsp;-reflecting a point in the y-axis or 

x-axis

&nbsp;-translating a point within and 

across quadrants

&nbsp;• Recognize, describe and perform 

transformations with triangles on a 

co-ordinate plane, focusing on the 

co-ordinates of the vertices when:

&nbsp;-reflecting a triangle in the x-axis or 

y-axis

&nbsp;-translating a triangle within and 

across quadrants

&nbsp;-rotating a triangle around the origin



#### &nbsp;Focus of transformations

&nbsp;• Doing transformations on a co-ordinate plane focuses attention on the co

ordinates of points and vertices of shapes.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that translations, reflections and rotations only 

change the position of the figure, and not its shape or size.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that the above transformations produce congruent 

figures.

&nbsp;• Learners do not have to learn general rules for the transformations at this 

stage, but should explore the way the co-ordinates of points change when 

performing different transformations with lines or shapes.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize that enlargements and reductions change the size 

of figures by increasing or decreasing the length of sides, but keeping the 

angles the same, produces similar rather than congruent figures.

&nbsp;• Learners should also be able to work out the factor of enlargement or 

reduction of a figure.

### Platonic solids

&nbsp;• Platonic solids are a special group of polyhedra that have faces that are 

congruent regular polygons. 

• There are only 5 Platonic solids:

&nbsp;-Tetrahedron

&nbsp;-Hexahedron (cube)

&nbsp;-Octahedron

&nbsp;-Dodecahedron

&nbsp;-Icosahedrons

&nbsp;• The name of each Platonic solid is derived from its number of faces.

&nbsp;• Platonic solids provide an interesting context in which to investigate the 

relationship between the number of faces, vertices and edges. By listing these 

properties for all the Platonic solids, learners can investigate the pattern that 

emerges, to come up with the general rule: V – E + F = 2, where V = number 

of vertices; E = number of edges; F = number of faces 



### Building 3d models

&nbsp;• Revise using nets to make models of 

geometric solids, including:

&nbsp;-cubes

&nbsp;-prisms

&nbsp;-pyramids



### using and constructing nets

&nbsp;• Using and constructing nets are useful contexts for exploring or consolidating 

properties of polyhedra.

&nbsp;• Learners should recognize the nets of different solids.

&nbsp;• Learners should make sketches of the nets using their knowledge of the 

shape and number of faces of the solids, before drawing and cutting out the 

nets to build models.

&nbsp;• Since learners have more knowledge about the size of angles in equilateral 

triangles, and can measure angles, their constructions of nets should be more 

accurate. 

• Learners have to work out the relative position of faces of the nets in order to 

build the 3D model.
