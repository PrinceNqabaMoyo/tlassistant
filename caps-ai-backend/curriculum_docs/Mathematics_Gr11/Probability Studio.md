## Probability

1. Revise the addition rule for mutually exclusive events:

&nbsp;   P(A or B)=P(A)+P(B),

&nbsp;  the complementary rule: P(not A)=1−P(A) and 

&nbsp;  the identity P(A or B) = P(A)+P(B)−P(A and B)



2\. Identify dependent and independent events and the product rule for independent events:

&nbsp;   P(A and B)=P(A)×P(B).

3\. The use of Venn diagrams to solve probability problems, deriving and applying formulae for any three events A, B and C in a sample space S.

4\. Use tree diagrams for the probability of consecutive or simultaneous events which are not necessarily independent.



&nbsp;Comment: 

• Venn Diagrams or Contingency tables can be used to study dependent and independent 

events.

&nbsp;examples:

&nbsp;1. P(A) = 0,45, P(B) = 0,3 and P(A or B) = 0,615. Are the events A and B mutually exclusive, 

independent or neither mutually exclusive nor independent?                                        

&nbsp;2. What is the probability of throwing at least one six in four rolls of a regular six sided die?        

&nbsp;3. In a group of 50 learners, 35 take Mathematics and 30 take History, while 12 take neither  

of the two. If a learner is chosen at random from this group, what is the probability that  

he/she takes both Mathematics and History?

&nbsp;18 reported relief from drugs B and C

&nbsp;68 reported relief from at least one of the drugs

&nbsp; 7 reported relief from all three drugs.
