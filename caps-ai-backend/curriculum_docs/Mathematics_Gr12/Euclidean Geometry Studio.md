### Foundations

* Types of triangles -

1. Scalene (All sides and angles are different);
2. Isosceles (Two sides are equal in length. The angles opposite the equal sides are also equal);
3. Equilateral- three sides are equal in length and all three angles are equal.);
4. Acute-angled (Each of the three interior angles is less than 90°;
5. Obtuse-angled (One interior angle is greater than 90°);
6. Right-angled (One interior angle is 90°).



* Conditions for Congruent triangles -

1. SSS (side, side, side);
2. SAS (side, incl. angle, side);
3. AAS (angle, angle, side);
4. RHS (90°, hypotenuse, side);
5. AAA (angle, angle, angle);
6. SSS (sides in prop.);

### Ratio and proportion

1. A ratio describes the relationship between two quantities which have the same units. We can use ratios to compare weights, heights, lengths, currencies, etc. A ratio is a comparison between two quantities of the same kind and has no units.



Example: if the length of a rectangle is 20 cm and the width is 60 cm, then we can express the ratio between the length and width of the rectangle as:



Length to width = 20 to 60 = 1 to 3



Or length/width = 20/60 = 1/3



Or length : width = 20 : 60 = 1 : 3



* The ratio of 1/3 describes the length of the rectangle relative to its width.
* A ratio written as a fraction is usually given in its simplest form.
* A ratio gives no indication of actual length. For example,

length/width = 50 cm/150 cm also gives a ratio of 1/3,



And length/width = 0,8 m /2,4 m also gives a ratio of 1/3



* Do not convert a ratio to a decimal (even though 1/3 and 0,3˙ have the same numerical value). \[The code needs to include the capacity to put a dot above a decimal digit to show recurrence.]





Proportionality describes the equality of ratios. If two or more ratios are equal to each other, then we say that they are in the same

proportion.



Consider 2 line segments, AC which has a point B along its length such that AB = x and BC = y, and line DF which has point E along its length such that DE = kx and EF = ky.



AB/BC = x/y = kx/ky = DE/EF

The line segments AB and BC are in the same proportion as DE and EF. The following statements are also true:

AB/BC = DE/FE and AB\*FE = BC\*DE

AB/AC = DE/DF  and AB\*DF = AC\*DE

BC/AC = EF/DF and BC\*DF = AC\*EF



We can also substitute x, y, kx, and ky to show algebraically that the statements are true.

For example,

BC . DF = y × (kx + ky)

= ky(x + y)

And AC . EF = (x + y) × ky

= ky(x + y)

∴ BC . DF = AC . EF

### Similarity of polygons



* Polygons are similar if they are the same shape but differ in size. In other words, one polygon is an enlargement of the other. Congruent polygons are also similar since they are the same shape and the same size, only their position or orientation is different.



* Two polygons with the same number of sides are similar when:

1. All pairs of corresponding angles are equal, and
2. All pairs of corresponding sides are in the same proportion.



* Important:

1. Both conditions must be true for two polygons to be similar.
2. If we are given two polygons that are similar, then we know that both conditions are true.



• The symbol for congruency is ≡.

• The symbol for similarity is |||.

### Similarity of triangles

To prove two triangles are similar, we need only show that only one of the conditions for similar polygons is true i.e that:

(a) all pairs of corresponding angles are equal or

(b) all pairs of corresponding sides are in the same proportion.



• Be careful to label similar triangles correctly.

For example, if angle P = angle B, angle Q = angle A and angle R = angle C, then Triangle PQR ||| Triangle BAC.

Do not write Triangle PQR ||| Triangle ABC.

• Triangle PQR ||| Triangle BAC also indicates which sides of the triangles are in the same proportion:

PQ/BA = QR/AC = PR/BC



* The sum of the interior angles of any triangle is 180°. If we know that two pairs of angles are equal, then the remaining angle in each triangle must also be equal. Therefore the two triangles are similar. In other words, to prove that two triangles are equiangular, we need only show that two pairs of angles are equal.
* To prove that the sides of two triangles are in the same proportion, we must show that all three pairs of corresponding sides are in the same proportion.

### Pythagorean theorem

* Many different methods of proving the theorem of Pythagoras have been formulated over the years. Similarity of triangles is one method that provides a neat proof of this important theorem.
* Converse: theorem of Pythagoras -If the square of one side of a triangle is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides of the triangle, then the angle included by these two sides is a right angle.



For any right-angled △MNP, if MQ is drawn perpendicular to NP, then:

△MNQ ||| △PMQ ⇒ MQ^2 = NQ . PQ

△MP Q ||| △NPM ⇒ MP^2 = QP . NP

△MNQ ||| △PNM ⇒ MN^2 = P N . QN

DIAGRAM \[Triangle ABC where ∠A is a right angle. Point D lies on BC. AD is drawn perpendicular to BC bisecting ∠A.]



Given:

△ABC with ∠A = 90°



Required to prove:

BC^2 = AB^2 + AC^2



PROOF

Construction: draw AD ⊥ BC.

∠C + ∠A\_2 = 90° (∠s of △CAD)

∠A\_1 + ∠A\_2 = 90° (given)

∴ ∠A\_1 = ∠C



∠A\_1 + ∠B = 90° (∠s of △ABD)

∴ ∠B = ∠A\_2

∠D\_1 = ∠D\_2 = ∠A = 90° (construction)



∴ 4ABD ||| 4CBA ||| △CAD (AAA)

∴AB/BC = BD/AB (△ABD ||| △CBA)

AB^2 = BD × BC





Similarly AC/CB = DC/AC      (△ABD ||| △CBA)

AC^2 = CB × DC

∴ AC^2 + AB^2 = (BD × BC) + (CB × DC)

= BC(BD + DC)

= BC(BC)

= BC^2

∴ BC^2 = AC^2 + AB^2
