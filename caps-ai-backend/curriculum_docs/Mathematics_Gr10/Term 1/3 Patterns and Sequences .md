## Describing patterns

* In earlier grades you saw patterns in the form of pictures and numbers. In grade 10, we learn more about the mathematics of patterns. Patterns are repetitive sequences and can be found in nature, shapes, events, sets of numbers and almost everywhere you care to look. For example, seeds in a sunflower, snowflakes, geometric designs on quilts or tiles, the number sequence 0; 4; 8; 12; 16; . . ..
* A sequence is an ordered list of items, usually numbers. Each item which makes up a sequence is called a “term”.
* To describe terms in a number pattern we use the following notation: The 1st term of a sequence is T1. The 4th term of a sequence is T4. The 10th term of a sequence is T10.
* The general term is often expressed as the nth term and is written as Tₙ.
* A sequence does not have to follow a pattern but, when it does, we can write down the general formula to calculate any term. For example, consider the following linear sequence: 1; 3; 5; 7; 9; . . .
* The nth term is given by the general formula: Tₙ = 2n − 1
* You can check this by substituting values into the formula: T1 = 2(1) − 1 = 1; T2 = 2(2) − 1 = 3; T3 = 2(3) − 1 = 5; T4 = 2(4) − 1 = 7; T5 = 2(5) − 1 = 9. If we find the relationship between the position of a term and its value, we can describe the pattern and find any term in the sequence.
* The common difference is the difference between any term and the term before it and is denoted by d. In some sequences, there is a constant difference between any two successive terms. This is called a common difference.
* To calculate the common difference, we find the difference between any term and the previous term: d = Tₙ − Tₙ₋₁
* It is important to note the difference between n and Tₙ. n can be compared to a place holder indicating the position of the term in the sequence, while Tn is the value of the place held by n.  So for n = 1, the value of T₁ = 4 and so on:

n, 1, 2, 3, 4, ⋯

Tₙ, 4, 6, 8, 10, ⋯

### Patterns and conjecture

* In mathematics, a conjecture is a mathematical statement which appears to be true, but has not been formally proven. A conjecture can be thought of as the mathematicians way of saying “I believe that this is true, but I have no proof yet”. A conjecture is a good guess or an idea about a pattern.
* For example, make a conjecture about the next number in the pattern 2; 6; 11; 17; . . . The terms increase by 4, then 5, and then 6. Conjecture: the next term will increase by 7, so it will be 17 + 7 = 24.



#### Examples

1. ##### Problem:

###### a. 

Investigate the type of number you get if you find the sum of an odd number and an even number.



###### b. 

Express your answer in words as a conjecture.



###### c. 

Use algebra to prove this conjecture.



##### SOLUTION

###### Step 1 : First try some examples

23 + 12 = 35

148 + 31 = 179

11 + 200 = 211



###### Step 2 : Make a conjecture

The sum of any odd number and any even number is always odd.



###### Step 3 : Express algebraically

Express the even number as 2x.

Express the odd number as 2y − 1.

Sum = 2x + (2y − 1)

= 2x + 2y − 1

= (2x + 2y) − 1

= 2(x + y) − 1

From this we can see that 2(x + y) is an even number. So then 2(x +

y) − 1 is an odd number. Therefore our conjecture is true.





##### 2\. Problem:

###### a. 

Consider the following examples of multiplying any two-digit number by 11.

What pattern can you see?

11 × 42 = 462

11 × 71 = 781

11 × 45 = 495



###### b. 

Express your answer as a conjecture.



###### c. 

Can you find an example that disproves your initial conjecture? If so, reconsider your conjecture.



###### d. 

Use algebra to prove your conjecture.



##### SOLUTION

###### Step 1 : Find the pattern

We notice in the answer that the middle digit is the sum of the two

digits in the original two-digit number.



###### Step 2 : Make a conjecture

The middle digit of the product is the sum of the two digits of the

original number that is multiplied by 11.



###### Step 3 : Reconsidering the conjecture

We notice that

11 × 67 = 737

11 × 56 = 616

Therefore our conjecture only holds true if the sum of the two digits is less than 10.



###### Step 4 : Express algebraically

Any two-digit number can be written as 10a + b. For example, 34 =

10(3) + 4. Any three-digit number can be written as 100a + 10b + c.



For example, 582 = 100(5) + 10(8) + 2.

11 × (10x + y) = 110x + 11y

= (100x + 10x) + 10y + y

= 100x + (10x + 10y) + y

= 100x + 10(x + y) + y

From this equation we can see that the middle digit of the three-digit number is equal to the sum of the two digits x and y.
