### Solving triangles

Not all triangles are right-angled triangles. We also have triangles that do not have a right 

angle. Such triangles are called oblique triangles.

In some oblique triangles each angle of the triangle has a value less than 90ЋЪ. We say such triangles are acute oblique triangles.

In other cases, one of the angles of a triangle has a value that is more than 90ЋЪ. Such a triangle are 

said to be an obtuse oblique triangles.



Note: Any tool for drawing/rendering triangle should be able to draw, right angled, acute oblique and obtuse oblique triangles



### The sine rule

For any triangle where a is the length of the side opposite angle A, b the 

length of the side opposite angle B and c the length of the side opposite angle C:



a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C  or  (sin A)/a  = (sin B)/b  = (sin C)/c



The sine rule is helpful when we have the following information about a triangle:

&nbsp;вАҐ the lengths of the two sides of the triangle and the size of an angle opposite one of the sides or

&nbsp;вАҐ the values of two angles and the length of one side



If we are given the lengths of two sides of a triangle and a non-included angle, we have three possibilities:

&nbsp;A. no triangle at all

&nbsp;B. one triangle possible

&nbsp;C. two triangles possible. This possibility is referred to as the ambiguous case.

### Solving trigonometric equations

The strategy we adopt is to find one solution 

using knowledge of special angles, and then use the symmetries in the graphs of the trigonometric functions to deduce additional solutions.



The values of the six trigonometric ratios cannot be calculated exactly for most angles. Nor can the exact value of an angle generally be found given the value of one of the ratios. 

There are, however, three special angles that lend themselves nicely to ratio calculation. They are 30ЋЪ, 45ЋЪ and 60ЋЪ.

Note: A table should be generated for the trig ratios of all special angles.

Notice that 30ЋЪ and 60ЋЪ angles are complementary and that a 45ЋЪ angle is its own complement.
