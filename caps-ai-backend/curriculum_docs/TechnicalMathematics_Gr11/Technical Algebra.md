### Laws and properties of exponents

1. For any non-zero number a and any real number m and n:  a^m × a^n = a^(m + n)
2. For any non-zero number a and any real numbers m and n: a^m/a^n  a(m-n)
3. For any real numbers m and n: (am)^n = a^(mn)
4. For any real number n: (ab)^n = (a^n)(b^n)
5. For any real number n: (a/b)^n = (a^n)/(b^n)
6. For any non-zero real number a: a^0 = 1
7. For any real number a, and any real number n: a^-n = 1/(a^n)

### Rational (fractional) exponents

By convention, when we use the square root symbol (√) in Mathematics, we are referring to the

positive square root only. This is called the principal square root. Thus, the √100 = 10,

because 10 is the positive square root of 100.

To convert from surd form to exponential form, use a fraction in the power form to indicate that it

stands for a root or a radical.

the 1/n are multiplied together, that is n√(a^m) = a^(m/n)

### Factorising expressions of the form ax^2 ± bx ± c

A quadratic equation is a polynomial of the form ax2 ± bx ± c,

where a, b and c are numbers. When factoring, you will find the two

numbers that will not only multiply to equal the constant term c,

but will also add up to equal b, the coefficient of the x-term.



When the coefficient of x^2 is 1 then look for factors that when multiplied give you the C value and that when added give you the coefficient of x.

When the coefficient of x^2 is other than 1 and not equal to 0, then you find the product of the c term and a term, then you look for factors of that product that add up to the value of the b term.

 



Factors will be:

a. (x + a)(x + b) if ab is positive and a + b is positive.

b. (x – a)(x – b) if ab is positive and a + b is negative.

c. (x + a)(x – b) if ab is negative, a + b is positive and a > b because (a + b) > 0

d. (x – a)(x + b) if ab is negative, a + b is negative and a < b because (a + b) < 0



Note:

 If the sign of the last term is a ‘+’, both signs are the same. To find out what these signs are, look

at the middle sign. If the middle sign is ‘+’ both signs will be ‘+’. If the middle sign is ‘–’, both

signs will be ‘–’.

 If the sign of the last term is ‘–’, the signs are different. If the middle sign is ‘+’, the sign in the

second bracket is ‘+’. If the middle sign is ‘–’, the sign in the second bracket is ‘–’.

### Solving quadratic equations using the quadratic formula

The quadratic formula:  x = \[-b ±  √(b^2 - 4ac)]/2a



One of the challenges we face when we use factorisation as a method of solving quadratic equations

is that it does not always work. Some quadratic equations are not factorable. Thus we need a method

that will always work.

The quadratic formula is derived by completing the square on the quadratic equation ax^2 + bx + c = 0.



 a(x^2)/a +  bx/a +  c/a = 0

 x^2 + (b/a)x + c/a = 0

 x^2 + (b/a)x + (b/2a)^2 - (b/2a)^2 + c/a = 0

 x^2 + (b/a)x +  b^2/4a^2 - b^2/4a^2 + c/a = 0

(x + b/2a)^2 - (b^2-4ac)/4a^2 = 0

(x + b/2a)^2 = (b^2-4ac)/4a^2

√\[(x + b/2a)^2] = ±√(b^2-4ac)/2a

x + b/2a = ±√(b^2-4ac)/2a

x = -b/2a ± √(b^2-4ac)/2a

### The discriminant: Δ= b^2 – 4ac

The discriminant, Δ= b^2 – 4ac, is derived from the quadratic formula, x = \[-b ±  √(b^2 - 4ac)]/2a

 Quadratic equation can have:

 • at most two real roots

 • two equal roots

 • no real root.

We use the value of the discriminant to determine the number and the nature of the roots of the

### Simultaneous equations in two unknowns

 Simultaneous equations are pairs of equations that have the same solution(s).

We will work with simultaneous equations in two unknowns, where one equation is quadratic and

the other is linear. For example, y = x2 – 4 and y = x – 2.

 Solving a system of simultaneous equations simply means calculating a value or values of x that

make(s) the two equations have the same value or values of y.



Example1:

Solve the given system of simultaneous equations:

y = x^2 – 4

y = x – 2



We are calculating a value or values of x that will give the same value of y when substituted in both equations.

x^2 – 4 = x – 2

x^2 – x – 4 + 2 = 0

x^2 – x – 2 = 0

(x – 2)(x + 1) = 0

x – 2 = 0 or x + 1 = 0

x = 2 or x = –1

When x = 2: y = 2^2 – 4 = 4 – 4 = 0

y = 2 – 2 = 0

If the value of x is 2 then y is zero and in coordinate form (2; 0).

When x = –1: y = (–1)^2 – 4 = 1 – 4 = –3

y = –1 – 2 = –3

What this means is that for the same value of x, the two equations have the same value of y. We write this solution in coordinate form as (–1; –3).

### Using critical points to draw the graph of f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c

When drawing the graph of f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c, we need to determine the following critical points:

• the x-intercept: Let y = 0 and then solve the quadratic equation using: a) factorisation, or b) the quadratic formula.

• the y-intercept: Let x = 0 and calculate c

• the axis of symmetry: x= -b/2a

• the coordinates of the turning point: By substituting x= -b/2a in f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c or by using \[-b/2a; c-(b^2)/4a)] to calculate the coordinates.

### An exponential graph: y = ab^x

&nbsp;An exponential function with base, b is defined by the function f(x) = ab^x, where a ≠ 0, b > 0 and  

b ≠ 1, and x is any real number. The base, b, is constant and the exponent, x, is a variable.

&nbsp;The features of an exponential graph are: 

• the domain is all Real numbers

&nbsp;• the range is all positive Real numbers (not zero)

&nbsp;• the graph has a y-intercept at (0; 1).

&nbsp;• when b > 1, the graph increases. The greater the base, b, the faster the graph rises from left  

to right

&nbsp;• when 0 < b < 1, the graph decreases

&nbsp;• has an asymptote (a line that the graph gets very, very close to, but never crosses or touches). 

For this graph the asymptote is the x-axis (y = 0).



Note:

 Any number to the power of zero equals 1.

### The equation of the circle with the center at the origin:  x^2 + y^2 = r^2

#### Writing the equation of a circle through a point and centred at the origin

If we take a circle and place it on the Cartesian plane so that its centre is at the origin, then we

can calculate its equation. The equation of a circle placed at the centre of the Cartesian plane is

x^2 + y^2 = r^2; where r is the radius and (x; y) is any point through which the circle passes.



##### Sketching circles given their equations

 Follow these guidelines for drawing the graph of a circle given its equation. To draw a graph of a

circle with its centre at the origin:

 1. first identify and label the origin as the center of the circle

 2. identify and label the two x-intercepts as (r; 0) and (–r; 0)

 3. identify and label the two y-intercepts as (0; r) and (0; –r)

 4. sketch the circle containing the intercepts



 The equation of the semi- circle with the center at the origin:  y = ±√(r^2 - x^2)

Consider the equation of the circle centred at the origin x^2 + y^2 = r^2. If we make y the subject of the formula, we get  y = ±√(r^2 - x^2)    . We can rewrite this equation as two separate equations:  y =  y = √(r^2 - x^2)  and   y = -√(r^2 - x^2)  . These are equations of the two halves of the circle, and each half is called a  semi-circle.
